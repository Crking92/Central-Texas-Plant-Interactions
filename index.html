How to read the scores

Scores are benchmark-normalized. The strongest documented target-zone plant is set to 100, and every other plant is compared to that standard. In the current dataset, Quercus virginiana is the benchmark. Ecological score shows direct animal-use signal. Balanced score adds evidence strength, target-zone confidence, native-status weighting, and non-native/invasive caution. A low score usually means verified data are thin for that category; it does not mean the plant has no ecological value.

About this project

Native plant food webs for Central Texas
Core idea: local native plants are usually the best first choice for habitat because they share long food-web history with local insects, birds, and mammals. Many native insects are picky eaters as larvae, and those insects become food for birds and other wildlife.

This dashboard compares plants by source-backed direct animal use: host use, pollen or nectar use, fruit, seed, nut, browse, nesting/cover use, and mammal plant-food records. It does not claim to show every interaction in nature. A blank or low score usually means this version has fewer verified records, not that the plant has no value.

  • Ecological score compares direct animal-use signal against the strongest documented target-zone plant.
  • Balanced score adds evidence quality, target-zone confidence, native-status weighting, and non-native or invasive caution.
  • Native target-zone plants receive a small recommendation advantage because the goal is Central Texas native habitat, not ornamental wildlife use alone.
  • Non-native plants can still show animal use, but their balanced score is reduced. Invasive or high-caution plants are strongly reduced because ecological harm can outweigh observed use.
  • Quercus virginiana is the current 100-point benchmark. Other plants are measured against that yardstick rather than scored by opinion.

Photos from iNaturalist are visual aids only. They are not used as scoring evidence.

Target-zone native logic

How planting-recommendation status is screened
Simple rule: a plant is treated as native to the target zone when it is documented from at least three counties in the City of Kyle target-zone area and its range reaches the south and west side of the Hays County region.

This rule keeps recommendations focused on plants that are not just Texas natives, but locally appropriate for the Edwards Plateau / Blackland Prairie transition around Kyle and Hays County. Review-only plants can still show animal interactions, but they are not treated as planting recommendations until target-zone status is checked.
